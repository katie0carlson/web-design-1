Frequently Asked Questions

HIV (Human Immunodeficiency Virus) is a blood-borne and sexually transmitted retrovirus that attacks the body’s immune system, primarily the cells that help fight infections (CD4+ T cells). Over time, if HIV is not treated with medication, the immune system becomes weaker and the body has a harder time fighting off infections and certain cancers. Untreated HIV infection typically progresses through stages: acute HIV infection (often with a flu‑like “acute retroviral syndrome”), a chronic or clinical latency phase (often asymptomatic but with ongoing viral replication), and eventually advanced disease. In 2024, an estimated 40.8 million people worldwide were living with HIV.

AIDS (Acquired Immunodeficiency Syndrome) is the most advanced stage of HIV infection. At this stage, the immune system is badly damaged and people are more likely to get serious infections or illnesses that a healthy immune system would normally control. Since the start of the epidemic, an estimated 44.1 million people have died from AIDS-related illnesses.

HIV spreads through unprotected sex, sharing injection equipment, and parent-to-child transmission—not through casual contact. Anyone can contract the virus, regardless of sexual orientation, race, gender, or age.

Prevention includes safer sex practices (using condoms), regular testing, PrEP/PEP, and never sharing needles/injection equipment.

HIV is now considered a manageable, chronic condition for most people who have access to modern treatment, especially antiretroviral therapy (ART). With today’s treatments, many people living with HIV never develop AIDS if they start and stay on effective medication. ART cannot cure HIV, but it can control the virus so well that many people living with HIV can expect a near‑normal life expectancy when they start treatment early and take it consistently. There are once‑daily pill regimens and, in some places, long‑acting injectable treatments, giving people different options to fit their lives and support adherence. Regular medical follow‑up, lab monitoring, and support for medication adherence are essential parts of care, helping treatment stay effective over the long term and reducing the chances of drug resistance.

Quick Reference: Key Concepts
Term Meaning
HIVSexually-transmitted retrovirus that weakens the immune system
AIDSAdvanced stage of HIV infection
PrEPMedication to lower HIV risk for people who are HIV-negative but are at high risk of contracting the virus
ART(Antiretroviral therapy) the combination of medicines that suppress HIV, protect the immune system, and prevent transmission.
